An underage lover, a crazy ex - but it was flirting with Stalin that really ruined Chaplin: Star accused of being a communist after saying 'purges' were 'wonderful' while he was harassed by former partner

By Peter Ackroyd

Published: 00:02 GMT, 8 April 2014 | Updated: 08:48 GMT, 8 April 2014

He was the Little Tramp who made the world laugh, but as the eminent biographer PETER ACKROYD reveals in a new book, Charlie Chaplin was a tortured genius who abused women and was haunted by his childhood poverty. Here, in the last part of our serialisation, Ackroyd tells how  America fell out of love with the star . . .

On the night of December 23, 1942, a young actress arrived unexpectedly at Charlie Chaplin’s mansion in Beverly Hills. He refused to see her. Undeterred, she waited till the early hours of the morning, stripped off her shoes and silk stockings, and climbed up a ladder.

On an upper floor, she found Chaplin.

Youth: Oona O'Neill, the wife of Charlie Chaplin,was just 17 years old when she met the film star

Pointing a gun at him, she announced she was going to commit suicide. His response? He talked to her for an hour and a half, and then took her to bed with him.

Afterwards, Chaplin joked that he’d never before had sex with a loaded gun at his head. ‘Well,’ he told the actress, ‘this is a new twist.’

In truth, 23-year-old Joan Barry was no stranger to him. They’d met the year before, when he was 52 and she was one of Hollywood’s many aspiring, pretty young actresses.

Somehow, he’d persuaded himself that she had talent and had put her under contract. By Joan’s own account, it was only then that his ‘pawing and mauling’ progressed to sexual relations. When she became pregnant, she said later, he persuaded her to have an abortion, even though she’d wanted to keep the child.

Chaplin was under few illusions about his latest lover. Joan would turn up drunk at his home at all hours of the night, and once smashed her car in the driveway.

Eventually, he refused to open the door to her, at which point she began breaking the windows. But their relationship continued. And a week or so after the loaded gun incident, a guard found her wandering through Chaplin’s garden, again with a gun in her hand.

He confiscated the weapon but, on the excuse of wanting to use the lavatory, she escaped through a downstairs window. A few hours later, she was found in the front seat of a car, wearing pyjamas, her lips smeared with iodine. A doctor diagnosed a ‘simulated suicide attempt’, and Joan was charged with vagrancy.

But still she kept harassing Chaplin, arriving at his home in May 1943 to confront him with the news that she was once again pregnant.

Honour: The pair were together when the silent film icon was knighted at Buckingham Palace in 1975

Having slipped in through the back door, she went upstairs to his bedroom, threw open the door — and saw a naked woman in his bed.

Joan promptly had hysterics and ran downstairs, where she tried — unsuccessfully — to slash her wrists. She was driven back to her hotel by Chaplin’s butler.

To the delight of his children, Chaplin would sometimes go behind the sofa and pretend that he was walking down some steps into the basement.

Before their eyes, he’d become smaller and smaller and smaller.

But it’s hard to say whether the Chaplin family was a happy one. Certainly, he approved of stern discipline, once remarking that children ‘must be prepared for life by some form of hardship’.

Any infraction of his rules would result in a spanking. His daughter Geraldine recalled that ‘we got the full routine, on the bum, over your father’s knee’.

Chaplin was also capable of viewing a child as a rival — and he became jealous of Michael, his wife’s favourite among their eight children.

Michael himself later said that Chaplin was ‘to put it mildly, a bit of a handful as a father’.

He added: ‘I never argued with the old man. I never dared to. In any case, it’s useless to argue with my father.

He is too stern, too inflexible, too overpowering.’ One visitor recalled that the children were ‘scared ****less’ of Chaplin and that he ‘corrected them all the time. Hence they gave him a  wide berth and did not speak to him if they could help it.’

At table, Chaplin ordered his sons to sit up straight and not speak unless spoken to.
For entertainment, he’d show them his early films in a private screening room. ‘There he is now, there comes the little fellow,’ he’d say, rubbing his hands with glee. ‘Watch it, boys.’

Chaplin was desperate to expunge the unstable actress from his life. Unexpectedly, he’d fallen in love with Oona O’Neill, the self-possessed and beautiful daughter of American playwright Eugene O’Neill.

It was Oona — just 17 when she met the ageing star — who had been the naked girl in Chaplin’s bed. Unlike many of his previous lovers, she had always enjoyed a privileged life. But she’d set her heart on becoming a film actress — and Chaplin had offered to give her acting lessons himself.

Before long, she’d moved into his home. The age difference was some 36 years, but Chaplin’s feelings for her were more powerful than any considerations of common sense.

As for Oona, according to one of Chaplin’s sons by a previous marriage: ‘She worshipped him — drinking in every word he spoke, whether it was about his latest script, the weather or some bit of philosophy.’

But when Chaplin decided to announce his engagement to Oona, who’d just turned 18, it was the final straw for her increasingly unstable predecessor, Joan.

A couple of weeks later, Joan’s mother, Gertrude, filed a suit on behalf of her unborn grandchild, naming Chaplin as the father and asking for $2,500 a month for the baby, and $10,000 for prenatal costs.

Worse still, the FBI became involved when they heard about her illegal abortions, and four months after Joan gave birth to a girl, Chaplin was charged with transporting his underage lover Oona across state lines for ‘immoral purposes’, and for conspiring with the Los Angeles police to have Joan imprisoned on charges of vagrancy.

He was facing a prison sentence of 20 years.

The trial opened on March 21, 1943. Hundreds of people lined the hallways of the courthouse to catch a glimpse of Chaplin on his arrival, and press photographers snapped him having his fingerprints taken.

Chaplin denied all Joan’s claims. His lawyer, Jerry Giesler, recalled that he ‘was the best witness I’ve ever seen in a law court. He was effective, even when he wasn’t being cross-examined but was merely sitting there, lonely and forlorn, at a far end of the counsel table.’

On April 4, to screams and tumult in the courtroom, Chaplin was acquitted of all charges.

But while the criminal case had gone well, the civil paternity suit went badly. Joan’s lawyer denounced Chaplin as a ‘grey-haired old buzzard’, ‘a little runt of a Svengali’, a ‘debaucher’ and ‘lecherous hound’ who ‘lies like a cheap Cockney cad’.

Turning to the jury, he said: ‘Wives and mothers all over the country are watching to see you stop him dead in his tracks.’

Home: After a turbulent experience in US courtrooms, the couple decided to move to Switzerland. By the start of 1953, the Chaplins had settled in a 15-room mansion (pictured) in Corsier-sur-Vevey, on Lake Geneva, where they led a largely secluded life

Though the case ended in deadlock, at a further trial the following year Chaplin was deemed to be responsible for the child’s welfare.

The judge awarded the infant girl, Carol Ann, the sum of $75 a week. And though Chaplin was by now happily married to Oona, his reputation fell into a steep decline.

To make matters far worse, he was being accused of being a communist.

At an Arts For Russia dinner in New York, he told guests that ‘the Russian purges’ were ‘a wonderful thing’ — adding that ‘the only people who object to communism and who use it as a bugaboo [an object of fear] are the Nazi agents in this country’.

Chaplin himself always claimed that he wasn’t a communist, and it’s difficult to believe that he had either the rigour or commitment to become one. For one thing, he made a great deal of money out of the stock market — and seemed the epitome of the successful self-made man.

Yet he did possess an angry instinct against injustice and oppression. No doubt thinking of his poverty-stricken childhood, he said once: ‘I’ve known humiliation. And humiliation is a thing you never forget.’

While facing paternity suits and accusations of being a Red, Chaplin embarked on a film about a mass murderer, Monsieur Verdoux. Perhaps not surprisingly, it contains a strain of deep misogyny, with female characters largely characterised as harsh, loud or foolish.

For the first time in his life, Chaplin lost money on a film. Indeed, scattered hissing and booing broke out at the close of an early screening. If he’d hoped to confront and quell his critics, he’d failed.

Family: Chaplin's son Michael poses in the living room of the Manoir de Ban where spent the final 25 years of his life

In 1947, a Congressman demanded Chaplin’s deportation on the ground that he was ‘detrimental to the moral fabric of America’.

Then he learned that he was being investigated by the notorious House Un-American Activities Committee. It was a highly dangerous time.

The committee had vigorously pursued anyone whom it considered to be disloyal or subversive, and most of its victims had ended up ostracised. In the film industry alone, 300 people were blacklisted or boycotted by the studios.

Chaplin now found himself a much lonelier figure. In restaurants, some customers would make loud and unfavourable comments about his perceived politics. Many of his old acquaintances no longer dared to visit him, fearing guilt by association. At a New Year’s Eve party, a guest actually spat in his face.

In 1952, no longer able to count upon a sympathetic reception in the U.S., he and his family embarked the Queen Elizabeth for the London premiere of his film Limelight.

He’d been only two days at sea when he was informed that his re-entry permit had been withdrawn: he could not return to the U.S. without being interrogated about his moral character and political affiliations.

The Attorney General of the U.S. told the Press that Chaplin ‘is in my judgment an unsavoury character’.

The comedian’s first response was one of defiance, but he was terrified the U.S. authorities would strip him of his assets. All his life, he’d expressed a horror of returning to the poverty he’d known as a child.

In London, he was greeted by a cheering crowd, although many believed that his reception was smaller than in previous years.

Action: In 1975, US Congressmen demanded that Chaplin be deported on the grounds that he was damaging the 'moral fabric' of America

Apart from anything else, Chaplin did not look anything like the Little Tramp any more. He was a small, silver-haired gentleman who was obliged to smile and be cheerful while worrying about his future.

Finally, he made up his mind. On November 17, Oona flew to Hollywood, where she emptied their safety deposit boxes, sent $4 million to foreign accounts, and had their furniture shipped to Europe.

In her absence, Chaplin suffered a nervous collapse. He feared that her plane might crash or that she might be detained by the American authorities: he simply could not envisage life without her.

On her return, he decided to move to Switzerland, where taxes were particularly low. By the start of 1953, the Chaplins had settled in a 15-room mansion in Corsier-sur-Vevey, on Lake Geneva, where they led a largely secluded life.

According to a close family friend, ‘Charlie came first for Oona. The children had to understand that.

‘She tried to make his exile pleasant. She entertained him at dinner — she was always a witty conversationalist — and she always made sure she looked her best.’

Another friend, the English actress Margaret Rutherford, observed that Oona’s ‘stillness and gentleness pervaded the room like pot-pourri. She rarely spoke, but you felt that she was there to protect her husband from any strain’.

In letters, Chaplin made it clear that he now despised what was once his adopted country. He made only two more films — bringing the total to 82 — and was often fearful that he wouldn’t be remembered.

However, after a turbulent love life, he had at least found a measure of peace. Oona cared for him devotedly until he died at the age of 88 on Christmas Day 1977, still basking in her selfless love.
