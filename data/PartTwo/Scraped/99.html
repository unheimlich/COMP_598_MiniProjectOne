As Bank of England hints that interest rates could rise by the end of the year: should borrowers lock into a fixed-rate mortgage now?

  • BofE governor says interest rates could rise by year-end
  • 56% of homeowners have an SVR or tracker mortgage
  • Experts believe fixing now could be the right move for many 

By Lee Boyce for Thisismoney.co.uk

Published: 07:31 GMT, 18 July 2015 | Updated: 07:31 GMT, 18 July 2015

40 shares

47

View
comments

Bank of England governor Mark Carney has hinted record low interest rates are set to rise from the 0.5 per cent they have been glued at since March 2009 – and this could mean mortgage rates have hit the bottom.

He said in a speech last night that households should prepare for rates to rise at 'around the turn of this year' – opening up the possibility of a rate hike before Christmas.

This could mean that record low mortgage rates could start to disappear from the market, while those with standard variable rate or tracker mortgages could see monthly costs go up.

Rate rise? If interest rates did rise from 0.5% that they are currently glued at, many could see their mortgage repayments go up

Currently, 44 per cent of homeowners have a fixed-rate mortgage, Bank of England statistics show. 

New homebuyers have taken advantage of record low fixed-rates for stability, figures reveal, with nine in ten choosing fixed rates.

However, when these two or five year terms finish, borrowers could be in for a nasty shock if rates do now steadily rise in the coming years.

The Bank of England statistics show the remaining 56 per cent of homeowners have either a SVR or tracker mortgage.

This means in theory, they would be hit by rates nudging up. It is believed the BofE would make small incremental rises of 0.25 per cent to moderate any pain felt by households.

But as the BofE cut rates to the 0.5 per cent level, many lenders didn't cut their SVRs at the same pace, meaning many still pay around five per cent a year.

Those on deals with this kind of rate are unlikely to see imminent rises in their mortgage payments, until there are bigger rises in base rate.

Slow and steady: How the Bank of England sees interest rates rising (Source This is Money / Bank of England)

According to figures from the Council for Mortgage Lenders, on an average outstanding home loan of £150,000 with ten years to run and an average rate of 3.11 per cent rising to 3.36 per cent, payments would go up £19, from £1,474 a month to £1,492.

This rises to £31 per month extra for those with £250,000 remaining with the same terms, and £6 per month more on those with £50,000 left.

Despite the attempt to wean many off of interest-only mortgages, there are still more than two million with one, the Financial Conduct Authority says.

Unlike repayments mortgages, these types of deals leave borrowers owing the original amount without chipping away at it.

These people are likely to have lower repayments, but would face larger rises in monthly payments, the CML says.

Using the same rates, a homeowner with a £50,000 mortgage would pay an extra £11 a month, if rates went up by 0.25 per cent. Someone on a £150,000 interest only mortgage would pay £31 more.

Lower for longer: The Bank's chart maps out how slowly rates are expected to rise compared to after previous recessions

It remains to be seen when rate rises come. Howard Archer, chief economist at IHS Global Insight, believes rises will be gradual.

He said: 'Regardless of whether the Bank of England first acts in late-2015 or early-2016, we see interest rates only rising to 1.25 per cent by the end of 2016 and two per cent by the end of 2017.'

This type of upwards movement in base rate would give people time to prepare for potentially higher mortgage bills.

But regardless, brokers are expecting a surge of remortgaging activity in the coming months. A study by Nottingham Building Society found 58 per cent of brokers expect a rise in remortgage business withing the coming months – and one in ten believe there will be a dramatic increase.

This rising confidence on remortgaging is supported by research that shows nearly one in five homeowners are considering switching deal in the next six months.

The largest group of homeowners contemplating this are those aged 25-34, many of whom may be in their first home and want to move onto better deals.

Many will be focusing their attention on fixed-rate mortgages. At the moment, the top two year rate comes from the Post Office at just 1.05 per cent (for those with a 40 per cent deposit) and Barclays is even offering a ten year deal with a 2.99 per cent rate.

For some, those may feel too short or too long, but five-year fixes at below 2.5 per cent might look a very tempting middle ground.

NatWest has a 2.23 per cent five-year rate for those looking to remortgage – but again, this requires a 60 per cent loan-to-value.

Mark Harris, chief executive of mortgage broker SPF Private Clients, said: 'There is no need to panic. Inevitably rates will rise at some point so it's important for borrowers to plan ahead.

'If you are on a variable rate, and would struggle to pay your mortgage if rates rose, it is worth locking into a fixed-rate.

'There are some really cheap deals on the market. In all likelihood, we have now seen the cheapest of these fixed rates so don't delay if you want one of the more competitive deals in the hope that a better rate will come along. It probably won't.'

Adrian Anderson, director of mortgage broker Anderson Harris, says: 'Once the money markets price in a rate rise, the cost of fixed rates start shooting up so if you want a fixed rate you may want to secure one quickly.

'Five-year fixes look particularly good value but don't fix for that long if there is a chance you might move in two or three years, or you'll be hit with a hefty penalty.

'Existing borrowers with spare cash not earning much in the way of interest may want to consider overpaying. This will put you in a stronger position when rates start to rise.'

David Hollingworth, of broker London and Country believes mortgage rates may even rise before a potential base rate hike.

He said: 'Borrowers can't expect to wait until the Monetary Policy Committee shows its hand and to still have a selection of the current ultra-low fixes at their disposal.

'Fixes will be higher at that point. It's useful to stress test your own finances to see how things might look as rates start to climb.'

But Ray Boulger, from broker John Charcol, thinks lenders have already factored in a potential base rate rise, so it is unlikely mortgage rates as soon as the announcement is made.

He said: 'Mortgage lenders are prepared for rates to go up and have taken this into account when setting their prices.

'In fact, they have factored in the expected rate rises over the next two to three years, which is why it is more expensive to take a 10-year fix than a two-year fix.'
